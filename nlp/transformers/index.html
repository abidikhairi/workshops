The Open Problem

Word2Vec gives every word one fixed vector regardless of context

"I went to the bank"
Financial institution
"Sitting on the river bank"
Edge of a river

Word2Vec → identical vector for both

Today's question: "How do we build a model that reads the whole sentence before deciding what each word means?"

The answer is attention — and it changes everything.

The Sequence Problem

Language has order — and order matters

"Dog bites man"

Routine news story

"Man bites dog"

Front page headline

Same words, opposite meanings — a model must respect word order

The Old Solution: RNNs

Recurrent Neural Networks process text word by word, left to right

The → trophy → didn't → fit → ... → it

Each step passes a "memory" to the next step

Analogy: reading a book one word at a time and trying to remember everything

Why RNNs Fall Short

"The trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big"
  • To resolve "it", the model needs to connect back to "trophy" — far away
  • Early words fade from memory — the vanishing gradient problem
  • Words must be processed sequentially — no parallelism, slow training
The insight: What if the model could look at all words at once and decide which ones matter for each position?
The vanishing gradient means that by the time the model reaches "it", the information about "trophy" has been diluted through many steps.

The Attention Idea

For each word, assign a relevance score to every other word

Use those scores to build a new, context-aware representation

The word "bank" in "river bank" will attend strongly to "river"

→ its meaning shifts accordingly

Session 1 Connection

Word2Vec averaged all contexts into one vector

Attention dynamically selects the relevant context at each position

Same word, different sentence → different representation

This is the conceptual bridge. Word2Vec was static — attention is dynamic. Make sure students feel the contrast before diving into mechanics.

Self-Attention Mechanics

Deep Dive

This is the core of the session. Take 20+ minutes here. Build up from intuition to equations step by step.

Three Roles: Q, K, V

Every word plays three roles simultaneously:

Query (Q)

"What am I looking for?"

Key (K)

"What do I offer to others?"

Value (V)

"What info do I actually give?"

Library Analogy

Q = your search query typed into the catalogue

K = the title/tags on each book

V = the actual content of each book

Attention = how well your query matches each book's tags → weighted sum of book contents

Creating Q, K, V — The Projection

Each word starts as an embedding vector $\mathbf{x}_i$ (from Session 1)

We project it into three separate spaces using learned weight matrices:

$$\mathbf{q}_i = W^Q \mathbf{x}_i, \quad \mathbf{k}_i = W^K \mathbf{x}_i, \quad \mathbf{v}_i = W^V \mathbf{x}_i$$

where $W^Q, W^K, W^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_k \times d}$ are weight matrices the model learns during training

         x_i (embedding)
        ╱      |      ╲
      W_Q     W_K     W_V
       ↓       ↓       ↓
      q_i     k_i     v_i
    (query)  (key)  (value)

Same word, three different views — the model learns what to ask, what to advertise, and what to share

Emphasize: Q, K, V are NOT the raw embeddings. They are learned linear projections. The same word becomes three different vectors, each serving a different purpose in the attention mechanism.

Step 1: Compute Attention Scores

How relevant is word $j$ to word $i$?

Measure with the dot product between query $i$ and key $j$:

$$\text{score}(i,j) = \mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_j = \mathbf{q}_i^\top \mathbf{k}_j$$

High dot product = vectors point in similar directions = high relevance

Example: "The cat sat"

Scores for the word "sat" (as query) against all words (as keys):

The cat sat
sat (Q) $\mathbf{q}_{\text{sat}} \cdot \mathbf{k}_{\text{The}}$ $\mathbf{q}_{\text{sat}} \cdot \mathbf{k}_{\text{cat}}$ $\mathbf{q}_{\text{sat}} \cdot \mathbf{k}_{\text{sat}}$
= 2.0 = 8.5 = 3.1

"cat" scores highest — most relevant to "sat"

Step 2: Scale the Scores

Raw dot products can be very large when vectors are high-dimensional

Large values → softmax outputs become almost 0 or 1 → gradients vanish

Fix: divide by $\sqrt{d_k}$ (the dimension of key vectors):

$$\text{scaled\_score}(i,j) = \frac{\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_j}{\sqrt{d_k}}$$

Continuing our example ($d_k = 64$, so $\sqrt{d_k} = 8$)

The cat sat
Raw scores 2.0 8.5 3.1
÷ $\sqrt{64}$ = 8 0.25 1.06 0.39

Think of it as keeping numbers in a comfortable range for the next step

If d_k = 64, random dot products have variance ~64. Dividing by sqrt(64) = 8 brings variance back to ~1. This is why it's called "scaled" dot-product attention.

Step 3: Softmax → Attention Weights

Convert scaled scores into a probability distribution (weights that sum to 1):

$$\alpha_{ij} = \text{softmax}_j\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_j}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) = \frac{\exp\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_j}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)}{\displaystyle\sum_{l=1}^{n} \exp\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_l}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)}$$

Continuing our example

The cat sat
Scaled scores 0.25 1.06 0.39
$e^{\text{score}}$ 1.28 2.89 1.48
$\alpha$ (weights) 0.23 0.51 0.26

Sum = 0.23 + 0.51 + 0.26 = 1.0   —   "sat" pays 51% of its attention to "cat"

The softmax is the same function from Session 1 (Word2Vec). Remind students: it turns any list of numbers into percentages.

Step 4: Weighted Sum of Values

Multiply each word's value vector by its attention weight, then sum:

$$\mathbf{z}_i = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \alpha_{ij} \, \mathbf{v}_j$$

For "sat":

$$\mathbf{z}_{\text{sat}} = 0.23 \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{The}} + \underbrace{0.51 \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{cat}}}_{\text{largest contribution}} + 0.26 \cdot \mathbf{v}_{\text{sat}}$$
$\mathbf{z}_{\text{sat}}$ is a new vector that blends information from the entire sentence, dominated by "cat" — the most relevant word

This is why it's called attention — the model attends to what matters and ignores the rest

The Full Attention Flow

  Input: x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ  (token embeddings)
         │   │        │
    ┌────┴───┴────────┴────────┐
    │   Linear projections     │
    │   Q = XW_Q               │
    │   K = XW_K               │
    │   V = XW_V               │
    └────┬───┬────────┬────────┘
         │   │        │
         Q   K        V
         │   │        │
    ┌────┴───┴────┐   │
    │  Q · K^T    │   │     ← dot product (similarity)
    │  ÷ √d_k    │   │     ← scale
    │  softmax    │   │     ← normalize to weights
    └──────┬──────┘   │
           │          │
         weights      V
           │          │
    ┌──────┴──────────┴──┐
    │  weights × V       │  ← weighted sum
    └────────┬───────────┘
             │
    Output: z₁, z₂, ..., zₙ  (context-aware vectors)
Walk through the diagram top to bottom. This is the single most important diagram of the session.

The Master Equation

Everything on the previous slides, in one formula:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

where $Q, K, V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k}$ are the matrices of all queries, keys, and values stacked together

What each piece does:

$QK^\top$ → $n \times n$ matrix of all pairwise similarity scores
$\div \sqrt{d_k}$ → stabilize the scale
softmax → normalize each row to attention weights
$\times V$ → weighted combination of value vectors
This is the equation from "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., 2017)

Worked Example — Tiny Numbers

Sentence: "I love NLP" — using $d_k = 2$ for simplicity

After projection (made-up values for illustration):

$$Q = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad K = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad V = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \\ 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 1 & 2: $\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{2}}$

$$QK^\top = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\div \sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 0.71 & 0 & 0.71 \\ 0.71 & 0.71 & 0 \\ 1.41 & 0.71 & 0.71 \end{bmatrix}$$

Step 3: Softmax each row (showing row 3: "NLP")

$$\text{softmax}([1.41,\; 0.71,\; 0.71]) = [0.49,\; 0.26,\; 0.26]$$

Step 4: Output for "NLP"

$$\mathbf{z}_{\text{NLP}} = 0.49 \cdot \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} + 0.26 \cdot \begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix} + 0.26 \cdot \begin{bmatrix}5\\6\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2.57\\3.58\end{bmatrix}$$
This is a full concrete walkthrough. Students can reproduce it with a calculator. The point: no magic, just matrix multiplication and softmax.

Why This Solves the Word2Vec Problem

"river bank"

$\alpha_{\text{bank,river}}$ is high

→ $\mathbf{z}_{\text{bank}}$ absorbs "river" info

→ shifts toward water/geography

"savings bank"

$\alpha_{\text{bank,savings}}$ is high

→ $\mathbf{z}_{\text{bank}}$ absorbs "savings" info

→ shifts toward finance

Same input embedding $\mathbf{x}_{\text{bank}}$, but different output $\mathbf{z}_{\text{bank}}$ depending on context

Static embeddings (Session 1): one vector per word, forever
Self-attention (Session 2): a new vector per word, per sentence

Demo — Visualizing Attention


from transformers import BertTokenizer, BertModel
import torch
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns

tokenizer = BertTokenizer.from_pretrained('bert-base-uncased')
model = BertModel.from_pretrained('bert-base-uncased', output_attentions=True)

sentence = "The trophy didn't fit because it was too big"
inputs = tokenizer(sentence, return_tensors='pt')
with torch.no_grad():
    outputs = model(**inputs)

# Extract attention from layer 0, head 0
attention = outputs.attentions[0][0, 0].numpy()

tokens = tokenizer.convert_ids_to_tokens(inputs['input_ids'][0])
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 8))
sns.heatmap(attention, xticklabels=tokens, yticklabels=tokens, cmap='Blues')

The heatmap is exactly the $\text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)$ matrix we derived!

Look at the "it" row — "trophy" lights up → $\alpha_{\text{it,trophy}}$ is high

This is the highlight demo. Give it space. The heatmap IS the attention weight matrix. Each row is one word's attention distribution over all other words. Connect it directly to the equations they just saw.

Multi-Head Attention

One attention head = one perspective, one "question" about each word

But language has multiple dimensions of relationships:

Syntactic

subject → verb

Semantic

pronoun → antecedent

Positional

nearby words

Multi-head attention = run attention in parallel, multiple times

Each head learns to focus on a different type of relationship

Analogy: instead of one expert reviewing a document, you have a committee — each reads for something different, then they combine their findings

Positional Encoding

Self-attention looks at all words simultaneously — no built-in sense of order

Problem: without fixing this, "dog bites man" = "man bites dog"
Same attention scores, different order

The Fix

Before feeding tokens in, add a position signal to each token's embedding

Think of it as stamping each word with its seat number before entering a lecture hall

Session 1 connection: Word2Vec embeddings captured meaning
Positional encodings add location — the Transformer needs both
Keep this brief — the intuition is enough. Students don't need to see the sinusoidal formula.

The Transformer Architecture

"Attention Is All You Need" — Vaswani et al., Google, 2017

Replaced RNNs almost entirely within two years

The title of the paper became one of the most famous in ML history.

Two Building Blocks

Encoder

Reads and understands input text

Produces rich contextual representations

Decoder

Generates output text token by token

Attends to the encoder's output

One Transformer Layer = Two Sub-layers

Multi-Head Self-Attention → Feed-Forward Network

Let every word look at every other word, then process each independently

Depth = Abstraction

Stack many layers — each builds higher-level understanding

Deep layers: abstract reasoning, discourse structure
Middle layers: semantic relationships, coreference
Layer 1: basic syntactic patterns (subject/verb agreement)

BERT has 12 layers, GPT-3 has 96 layers, GPT-4 is estimated to have even more

Three Model Families

Model Architecture Used For
BERT Encoder only Understanding (classification, Q&A)
GPT Decoder only Generation (text, code, conversation)
T5, BART Encoder + Decoder Translation, summarization
These three families cover nearly all modern NLP. We'll focus on GPT (decoder-only) for the deep dive.

GPT Architecture

Deep Dive

GPT = Generative Pre-trained Transformer

Decoder-only stack with causal (masked) self-attention

This is the second deep-dive section. Take 20 minutes here.

What Makes GPT a Decoder?

Key constraint: each token can only attend to tokens before it — never the future

BERT (Encoder)

Sees the full sentence in both directions

"The [MASK] sat on the mat"

GPT (Decoder)

Can only look left — no peeking ahead

"The cat sat ___"

Why? GPT is trained to predict the next word — looking ahead would be cheating

Layer-by-Layer Anatomy


Input text: "The cat sat"
       ↓
[1] Token Embedding      ← look up each token's vector
       ↓                   (learned, like Word2Vec)
[2] Positional Encoding  ← add position signal
       ↓
[3] × N Transformer Blocks:
    ├ Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention
    ├ Layer Normalization
    ├ Feed-Forward Network
    └ Layer Normalization
       ↓
[4] Output Projection    ← map to vocabulary size
       ↓
[5] Softmax              ← probability over all words
       ↓
Predicted next token: "on" (0.43), "near" (0.12), ...

Step [1] = Session 1's Word2Vec job, but learned jointly with everything else

Walk through each step. Connect [1] to Word2Vec from Session 1, [2] to positional encoding from Slide 6, [5] to softmax from Session 1.

Autoregressive Generation

GPT generates one token at a time, appending each before predicting the next

"The" → "cat" → "sat" → "on" → "the" → "mat"

Temperature

Low (0.1)

Always picks most likely token

Repetitive, safe

High (1.5)

Samples more randomly

Creative, sometimes incoherent

The model has no "plan" — it just keeps predicting the next most likely token

Demo — GPT Text Generation


from transformers import GPT2Tokenizer, GPT2LMHeadModel
import torch

tokenizer = GPT2Tokenizer.from_pretrained('gpt2')
model = GPT2LMHeadModel.from_pretrained('gpt2')

prompt = "Natural language processing allows computers to"
inputs = tokenizer(prompt, return_tensors='pt')

# Generate with different temperatures
for temp in [0.3, 1.0, 1.5]:
    output = model.generate(
        **inputs,
        max_new_tokens=30,
        temperature=temp,
        do_sample=True,
        pad_token_id=tokenizer.eos_token_id
    )
    generated = tokenizer.decode(output[0], skip_special_tokens=True)
    print(f"\n[Temperature {temp}]")
    print(generated)

Pre-training & Fine-tuning

This section explains how models go from random weights to ChatGPT. Take 10 minutes here.

Pre-training: Learning Language

Train GPT on massive unlabeled text: Wikipedia, books, Common Crawl

Objective: predict the next word — forces the model to learn grammar, facts, reasoning

No human labels needed — the text itself is the supervision signal

Session 1 Connection

Word2Vec learned from co-occurrence in a local window

GPT pre-training learns from next-word prediction across entire documents

Same core intuition — "meaning comes from context" — applied at massive scale

Fine-tuning: Specializing

Take the pre-trained model → continue training on a small, task-specific dataset

  • Medical Q&A: fine-tune on doctor-patient dialogues
  • Sentiment analysis: fine-tune on product reviews with star ratings
  • Code generation: fine-tune on GitHub repositories

Analogy

Pre-training = 20 years of school and reading → broad knowledge

Fine-tuning = 3 months of on-the-job training → specialized skill

You don't re-learn to read for every new job — you build on what you know

From GPT to ChatGPT

GPT (pre-trained) → Fine-tune on dialogues → RLHF

RLHF = Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

  • Human raters score model responses
  • Train a reward model from those scores
  • Fine-tune GPT to maximize the reward
This shifts the model from "predict likely text"
to "give helpful, honest answers"
