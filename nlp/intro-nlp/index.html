The Vocabulary Problem

A model can only work with words it has seen before

One-Hot Encoding

Each word = a vector of 0s with a single 1

$$\mathbf{x}_{\text{cat}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{x}_{\text{dog}} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{x}_{\text{sat}} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
Every pair is equally distant — the model is blind to meaning

Why One-Hot Fails — The Math

For any two different one-hot vectors $\mathbf{x}_i$ and $\mathbf{x}_j$:

$$\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j = 0 \quad \text{(always orthogonal)}$$
$$\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\| = \sqrt{2} \quad \text{(always the same distance)}$$
$d(\text{cat}, \text{dog}) = d(\text{cat}, \text{democracy}) = \sqrt{2}$

No notion of similarity is possible — we need a denser representation

This is the core limitation. The model treats every word as equally different from every other word. We need a better representation.

The Distributional Hypothesis

"A word is known by the company it keeps"
— J.R. Firth, 1957

Words appearing in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings

Not a formula — a linguistic observation that turns out to be computationally powerful

Don't rush this slide. The distributional hypothesis is the conceptual core of the session. If students get this, everything else follows.

Co-occurrence Table

hot cold drink morning bark
coffee ✓ ✓ ✓
tea ✓ ✓ ✓ ✓
dog ✓
  • "coffee" and "tea" share many contexts → close in meaning
  • "dog" shares none → far away
We can learn meaning from co-occurrence patterns alone — no labels needed

From Co-occurrence to Geometry

Represent each word as a vector of co-occurrence counts

$$\mathbf{v}_{\text{coffee}} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\1\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_{\text{tea}} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\\1\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_{\text{dog}} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}$$

We measure similarity with cosine similarity:

$$\cos(\theta) = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{\|\mathbf{a}\| \; \|\mathbf{b}\|}$$
$\cos(\text{coffee}, \text{tea}) = \frac{1+0+1+1+0}{\sqrt{3}\;\sqrt{4}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{12}} \approx 0.87$
$\cos(\text{coffee}, \text{dog}) = \frac{0}{\sqrt{3}\;\sqrt{1}} = 0$

This is the bridge from raw counting to learned embeddings

Walk through the cosine similarity calculation step by step. The key insight: similar co-occurrence patterns → high cosine → close in meaning.

Word Embeddings — The Key Idea

Instead of sparse one-hot vectors, use dense low-dimensional vectors

$$\mathbf{x}_{\text{one-hot}} \in \{0,1\}^{|V|} \quad \longrightarrow \quad \mathbf{e}_{\text{embedding}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$$

where $|V| \approx 50{,}000$ (vocabulary size) and $d \approx 100\text{–}300$ (embedding dimension)

Similar words → geometrically close vectors

These are not hand-crafted — the model learns them entirely from data

Vector Arithmetic on Meaning

$$\vec{\text{king}} - \vec{\text{man}} + \vec{\text{woman}} \approx \vec{\text{queen}}$$

Why does this work? The embedding space encodes directions as concepts:

$$\vec{\text{king}} - \vec{\text{queen}} \approx \vec{\text{man}} - \vec{\text{woman}} \approx \vec{\text{gender}}$$

The "royalty" direction and "gender" direction are separate axes in the geometry

We find the answer by: $\underset{w \in V}{\arg\max} \; \cos\!\big(\mathbf{e}_w, \; \vec{\text{king}} - \vec{\text{man}} + \vec{\text{woman}}\big)$

Word2Vec

How It Works

This is the deep-dive section. Take 20 minutes here.

Two Training Strategies

CBOW

Continuous Bag of Words

Given surrounding words, predict the center word

["the", "cat", ___, "on", "mat"]
→ predict "sat"

Skip-gram

 

Given the center word, predict surrounding words

"sat"
→ predict ["the", "cat", "on", "mat"]

Both use a shallow neural network with one hidden layer

The hidden layer weights = the word embeddings

Skip-gram Objective

Given a center word $w_t$, maximize the probability of its context words:

$$J(\theta) = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{\substack{-m \le j \le m \\ j \ne 0}} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)$$

$T$ = number of words in the corpus, $m$ = window size (e.g. 2)

The probability is computed with a softmax:

$$P(w_o \mid w_c) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{u}_{w_o}^{\top} \mathbf{v}_{w_c})}{\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(\mathbf{u}_{w}^{\top} \mathbf{v}_{w_c})}$$

$\mathbf{v}_{w_c}$ = embedding of center word, $\mathbf{u}_{w_o}$ = embedding of context word

Read this as: "the probability that we see word w_o in the context of word w_c is proportional to how similar their vectors are." The dot product measures similarity — softmax turns it into a probability.

Softmax — Intuition

The softmax converts raw scores (dot products) into probabilities:

$$\text{softmax}(z_i) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{k=1}^{|V|} e^{z_k}}$$
  • High dot product $\mathbf{u}_o^\top \mathbf{v}_c$ → high probability (words likely co-occur)
  • Low dot product → low probability (words unlikely together)
Problem: the denominator sums over the entire vocabulary ($|V| \approx 50{,}000$)
This is expensive — Word2Vec uses negative sampling to approximate it

Negative Sampling

Instead of computing the full softmax, sample $k$ random "negative" words:

$$J_{\text{neg}} = \log \sigma(\mathbf{u}_{o}^{\top} \mathbf{v}_{c}) + \sum_{i=1}^{k} \mathbb{E}_{w_i \sim P_n(w)} \left[ \log \sigma(-\mathbf{u}_{w_i}^{\top} \mathbf{v}_{c}) \right]$$

$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ is the sigmoid function, $k \approx 5\text{–}20$

  • Push up the score of the real context word $(w_o, w_c)$
  • Push down the score of $k$ random words that were not in the context
This is the core training loop — after millions of push/pull updates,
similar words end up with similar vectors
The key insight: instead of making the model score ALL words, just make it distinguish real context from random noise. Much faster, nearly as good.

Training Signal Intuition

  • The model is never told what words mean
  • It only sees: "these words appear near each other" vs "these words don't"
  • After millions of examples, the geometry of the embedding space encodes semantics
Algebra analogy: the model solves a system of constraints — words that share many constraints end up close together

Demo — Similarity & Analogies


# Word similarity
wv.similarity("cat", "dog")        # → 0.922
wv.similarity("cat", "democracy")  # → 0.079

# Nearest neighbors
wv.most_similar("coffee", topn=3)  # → tea, drinks, drink

# Vector arithmetic: king - man + woman ≈ ?
wv.most_similar(positive=["king", "woman"], negative=["man"], topn=3)
# → queen, monarch, princess
Same idea as one-hot — but now cat/dog are close and cat/democracy are far

Demo — More Analogies & Odd One Out


# Geography analogy: paris - france + germany ≈ ?
wv.most_similar(positive=["paris", "germany"], negative=["france"])
# → berlin

# Which word doesn't belong?
wv.doesnt_match(["cat", "dog", "fish", "car"])  # → car

The model was never taught geography or animal categories.
It learned them purely from word co-occurrence patterns.

This is the payoff moment. These results feel almost magical and directly validate the distributional hypothesis from earlier.

Limits of Static Embeddings

Every word gets one fixed vector regardless of context

"I went to the bank"
💰 Financial institution
"Sitting on the river bank"
🌊 Edge of a river

Word2Vec → identical vector for both

Remember our question: "Does Word2Vec know which 'bank' we mean?"
No. It averages all contexts into one point.

This is exactly what Transformers solve → Session 2

Recap

  1. Language = structured system with syntax, semantics, pragmatics — rich and deeply ambiguous
  2. Pipeline: Text → tokens → vocabulary → representation → model
  3. One-hot encoding = blind to meaning
  4. Distributional hypothesis: meaning emerges from context patterns
  5. Word2Vec: learn embeddings by predicting context — no labels needed
  6. Remaining gap: static vectors have no context-sensitivity
